\begin{abstract}
We develop a polynomial representation for parity sequences of generalised
Collatz-type maps $(g,h,q)$, working in the ring $\ZZ[g,h]$ rather than
specialising immediately to numerical values.  The central object is the
\emph{structural polynomial} $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ attached to a parity sequence,
which encodes the accumulated additive contribution of the odd steps
independently of the parameter $q$.

The main results are: (1) a \emph{path identity} $h^e b = g^o a + q\kpoly_w(g,h)$
valid for any parity sequence; (2) a \emph{geometric polynomial theorem} showing
that Steiner words $\St(\alpha,\beta)$ have $\kpoly(g,h) = (g^\alpha -
h^\alpha)/(g-h)$, independent of $\beta$; (3) a \emph{composition rule}
$\kpoly_{Q\circ P} = g^{o_Q}\kpoly_P + h^{e_P}\kpoly_Q$; and (4) a
\emph{cycle element identity} $(h^e - g^o)\cdot a = q\cdot\kpoly_w(g,h)$
giving the fixed point in terms of $(g,h,q)$.

Beyond these core results, the path identity is read as a congruence
constraint, yielding an \emph{affine path family} parameterised by an
\emph{admissibility residue} $r$; cycles arise precisely when this family
intersects the diagonal $a = b$.  Integer solutions to the cycle element
identity are classified into four \emph{cycle flavours} (natural, reduced,
negative, rational), each an independently defined normalisation of the
solution pair $(a,q)$.  We also develop a system of five equivalent
representations of any parity sequence --- its bits (the $p$-value), the
structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$, its specialisation $\kpoly_w(3,2)$,
the $\sigma$-polynomial $\sigma_w(u,v)$, and the orbit parity sequence $\phi_w$ ---
connected by explicitly invertible transformations.  We introduce a
successor operation on $\kpoly_w$ in $\ZZ/(h^e-g^o)\ZZ$ that generates all
cycle representatives by rotation, and we warn that polynomial
non-divisibility of $h^e - g^o \nmid \kpoly_w$ in $\ZZ[g,h]$ does not
preclude pointwise divisibility at specific integer bases.  A concluding
section reflects on what the abstract path framework reveals and what it
cannot settle.
Applications to the $2a+1$ composition identity are developed in the companion
paper~\cite{paper73}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------

A \emph{generalised Collatz-type map} with basis $(g,h,q)$ consists of two
elementary operations on the positive integers: an \emph{odd step}
$x \mapsto gx + q$ and an \emph{even step} $x \mapsto x/h$.  The standard
Collatz map (the Syracuse odd-only iteration) uses basis $(g,h,q) = (3,2,1)$;
the $5x+1$ analogue uses $(5,2,1)$.  A \emph{parity sequence} is a finite
word over $\{O, E\}$ specifying the order in which these steps are applied.
Each parity sequence acts as an affine map on its domain of definition.

The central algebraic object we study is the \emph{structural polynomial}
$\kpoly(g,h) \in \ZZ[g,h]$ attached to a parity sequence.  It captures the
additive part of the parity sequence's affine action in a basis-independent
form.  The key advantage of working in $\ZZ[g,h]$ rather than substituting
$g = 3$, $h = 2$ at the outset is that polynomial identities can be proved
and, just as importantly, their \emph{failure for other bases} can be
identified precisely.  A numerical check that an identity holds at $(3,2)$
gives no information about why it fails at $(5,2)$; working symbolically
makes the obstruction explicit.

The power of the symbolic approach is illustrated dramatically by the $5x+1$
system, which possesses a non-trivial cycle $17 \to 43 \to 27 \to 17$ under
the Syracuse $5x+1$ map.  Via the cycle element identity (Section~\ref{sec:cycle}),
the fixed point $a = 17$ is determined by
$(h^e - g^o)\cdot a = q\cdot\kpoly(g,h)$: with $\kpoly(5,2) = 51$ and
$h^7 - g^3 = 128 - 125 = 3$, we get $3 \cdot a = 51$, giving $a = 17$.  The near-miss
$h^e - g^o = 3$ is the arithmetic miracle that makes the cycle possible, and
it is visible as a polynomial phenomenon.

The paper is organised as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:path} establishes the
path identity and defines the structural polynomial.  Section~\ref{sec:steiner}
proves the geometric polynomial theorem for Steiner words.
Section~\ref{sec:composition} derives the composition rule.
Section~\ref{sec:affine-paths} develops the affine path family, the
admissibility residue, and their intersection with cycles.
Section~\ref{sec:cycle} develops the cycle element identity as an algebraic
object: natural and reduced encodings, the uniform factor lemma, the successor
operation, and a proposition cautioning that polynomial non-divisibility does
not imply pointwise non-divisibility.
Section~\ref{sec:cycle-flavours} classifies cycle encodings into four flavours
(natural, reduced, negative, rational).
Section~\ref{sec:pvalue} develops the five representations of a parity
sequence --- the orbit parity sequence $\phi_w$, the $p$-value, the
$\sigma$-polynomial, $\kpoly_w(g,h)$, and $\kpoly_w(3,2)$ --- and unifies
them in a single diagram.
Section~\ref{sec:examples} collects worked examples illustrating the theory.
Section~\ref{sec:abstract-cycle} reflects on what the abstract path framework
reveals and what it leaves open.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Structural Polynomial and Path Identity}
\label{sec:path}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[Basis and elementary maps]
A \emph{basis} is a triple $(g, h, q)$ of integers with $g, h \geq 1$ and
$q \neq 0$.  The associated elementary maps are the \emph{odd step}
$O\colon x \mapsto gx + q$ and the \emph{even step} $E\colon x \mapsto x/h$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Parity sequence]
A \emph{parity sequence} is a finite word $w = w_1 w_2 \cdots w_n$ (possibly
empty, $n \geq 0$) over the alphabet $\{O, E\}$.  It defines an affine map
$\Phi_w$ by composing the corresponding elementary maps from left to right:
$\Phi_w = \Phi_{w_n} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_{w_1}$ (the empty composition
is the identity map).  We write $o = o(w)$ for the number of occurrences of
$O$ in $w$ and $e = e(w)$ for the number of occurrences of $E$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Throughout this paper we assume $h \mid x$ whenever an $E$-step is applied,
so that all maps are defined on their natural domain.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[$\kappa$-tuple]
\label{def:eprofile}
Let $w$ be a parity sequence with $o \geq 1$ odd steps and $e$ even steps.
Number the $O$-steps from $0$ to $o-1$ in the order they appear in $w$.
For $0 \leq j \leq o-1$, let $\kappa_j$ denote the number of $E$-steps that
appear in $w$ \emph{strictly before} the $(j+1)$-th $O$-step.  The sequence
$(\kappa_0, \kappa_1, \ldots, \kappa_{o-1})$ is the \emph{$\kappa$-tuple}
of $w$.  It satisfies $0 \leq \kappa_0 \leq \kappa_1 \leq \cdots \leq \kappa_{o-1} \leq e$.
For example, $w = OEEOEOEE$ has $o = 3$ odd steps at positions $0, 3, 5$,
preceded by $0$, $2$, $3$ even steps respectively, so $\kappa = (0, 2, 3)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
For the standard Collatz basis ($g=3$, $q=1$), a parity sequence beginning
with an $O$-step (i.e.\ $\kappa_0 = 0$) corresponds to a \emph{legal} orbit
(one driven by the actual parity of each element, not by the letters of $w$)
starting from an odd integer if and only if its $\kappa$-tuple is strictly
increasing
(equivalently, no two $O$-steps are adjacent).  The polynomial framework works
unchanged for non-strict profiles (see Example~\ref{ex:noncollatz}).
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Structural polynomial]
\label{def:structpoly}
The \emph{structural polynomial} of a parity sequence $w$ with
$\kappa$-tuple $(\kappa_0, \ldots, \kappa_{o-1})$ is
\[
  \kpoly_w(g,h) \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{o-1} g^{o-1-j}\, h^{\kappa_j}
  \;\in\; \ZZ[g,h].
\]
When $o = 0$, we set $\kpoly_w = 0$.
\end{definition}

We can now state and prove the central algebraic identity.

\begin{theorem}[Path identity]
\label{thm:path}
Let $(g,h,q)$ be a basis and $w$ a parity sequence with $o$ odd steps and
$e$ even steps, having structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$.  If
$b = \Phi_w(a)$ then
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:path}
  h^e \cdot b \;=\; g^o \cdot a \;+\; q \cdot \kpoly_w(g,h).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We proceed by induction on the length $n = |w|$ of the parity sequence.

\textit{Base cases.}
If $w = O$ (single odd step), then $e = 0$, $o = 1$, $b = ga + q$, and
$\kpoly_w = g^0 h^0 = 1$.  The identity reads $b = ga + q\cdot 1$, which
holds.  If $w = E$ (single even step), then $o = 0$, $e = 1$, $b = a/h$,
$\kpoly_w = 0$, and the identity reads $h \cdot (a/h) = a$, which holds.

\textit{Inductive step.}  Suppose~\eqref{eq:path} holds for all parity
sequences of length less than $n$, and write $w = w' \cdot \sigma$ where
$\sigma \in \{O, E\}$ is the last step.  Let $c = \Phi_{w'}(a)$ be the
intermediate value, so $b = \Phi_\sigma(c)$.  By the inductive hypothesis,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:ih}
  h^{e'} \cdot c = g^{o'} \cdot a + q \cdot \kpoly_{w'}(g,h),
\end{equation}
where $o' = o(w')$ and $e' = e(w')$.

\textit{Case $\sigma = E$.}  Then $b = c/h$, $o = o'$, and $e = e' + 1$.
Since $h^{e'+1}(c/h) = h^{e'}c = g^{o'}a + q\kpoly_{w'}$ by~\eqref{eq:ih},
and the E-step appended at the end does not precede any $O$-step, so the
$\kappa$-tuple of $w$ equals that of $w'$, and therefore
$\kpoly_w = \kpoly_{w'}$.  The identity~\eqref{eq:path} holds.

\textit{Case $\sigma = O$.}  Then $b = gc + q$, so $o = o' + 1$ and $e = e'$.
The new $O$-step is the last (index $j = o' $) in $w$.  All $e' = e$
even steps in $w$ precede it (they all belong to $w'$), so $\kappa_{o'} = e'$.
The structural polynomial of $w$ is
\[
  \kpoly_w(g,h) = g\cdot\kpoly_{w'}(g,h) + h^{e'},
\]
because the exponents of the first $o'$ terms each gain one extra factor of
$g$, and the new final $O$-step contributes $g^0 h^{e'} = h^{e'}$.
Now compute:
\begin{align*}
  h^e \cdot b &= h^{e'}\cdot(gc + q) \\
              &= g \cdot h^{e'} c + q \cdot h^{e'} \\
              &= g\bigl(g^{o'} a + q\cdot\kpoly_{w'}(g,h)\bigr) + q\cdot h^{e'}
                 \quad\text{(by \eqref{eq:ih})} \\
              &= g^{o'+1} a + q\bigl(g\cdot\kpoly_{w'}(g,h) + h^{e'}\bigr) \\
              &= g^o \cdot a + q \cdot \kpoly_w(g,h). \qedhere
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:trailing}
Two features of the path identity deserve emphasis.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ is independent of $q$: the parameter
        $q$ appears only as a uniform scalar multiplier.  Thus the additive
        structure of parity sequences is entirely captured by the polynomial
        ring $\ZZ[g,h]$.
  \item The polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ is independent of trailing $E$-steps:
        appending $E$-steps at the end only scales $h^e$ on the left-hand
        side of~\eqref{eq:path}, leaving $\kpoly_w$ unchanged.  This means
        $\kpoly_w$ alone does not uniquely identify a parity sequence; the
        $p$-value (Section~\ref{sec:pvalue}) resolves the ambiguity by
        appending a sentinel bit that records the length $n$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\section{Steiner Words and the Geometric Polynomial Theorem}
\label{sec:steiner}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[Steiner word]
\label{def:steiner}
For integers $\alpha \geq 1$ and $\beta \geq 0$, the \emph{Steiner word}
$\St(\alpha, \beta)$ is the parity sequence
\[
  \St(\alpha,\beta) \;=\; \underbrace{(OE)^{\alpha}}_{\alpha \text{ copies of } OE}
  \underbrace{E^\beta}_{\beta \text{ extra } E\text{-steps}}.
\]
It has $o = \alpha$ odd steps and $e = \alpha + \beta$ even steps.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Steiner words are the natural building blocks of Collatz orbits under the
Syracuse (odd-only) map: each Syracuse step $S(a) = (3a+1)/2^{v_2(3a+1)}$
corresponds to a Steiner word $\St(1,\beta)$ where $\beta = v_2(3a+1) - 1
\geq 0$.  A block of $\alpha$ consecutive applications of $S$ before the next
element divisible by an extra power of $h$ corresponds to $\St(\alpha,\beta)$.
\end{remark}

The $\kappa$-tuple of $\St(\alpha,\beta)$ has a particularly simple form.

\begin{lemma}[$\kappa$-tuple of a Steiner word]
\label{lem:profile}
The $\kappa$-tuple of $\St(\alpha,\beta)$ is $\kappa_j = j$ for
$0 \leq j \leq \alpha - 1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
In $(OE)^\alpha E^\beta$, the $j$-th $O$-step (zero-indexed) appears at
position $2j+1$.  Exactly $j$ $E$-steps appear before it (one after each of
the first $j$ $O$-steps).  Hence $\kappa_j = j$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric polynomial theorem]
\label{thm:geometric}
For any $\alpha \geq 1$ and $\beta \geq 0$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:geometric}
  \kpoly_{\St(\alpha,\beta)}(g,h) \;=\; \frac{g^\alpha - h^\alpha}{g - h}
  \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{\alpha-1} g^{\alpha-1-j} h^j.
\end{equation}
In particular, $\kpoly_{\St(\alpha,\beta)}$ is independent of $\beta$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Substituting $\kappa_j = j$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:profile} into
Definition~\ref{def:structpoly}:
\[
  \kpoly_{\St(\alpha,\beta)}(g,h)
  = \sum_{j=0}^{\alpha-1} g^{\alpha-1-j} h^j
  = \frac{g^\alpha - h^\alpha}{g-h},
\]
where the last equality is the standard geometric series identity.
Independence of $\beta$ follows from Remark~\ref{rem:trailing}.
\end{proof}

\section{Composition of Structural Polynomials}
\label{sec:composition}
%------------------------------------------------------------

If $P$ and $Q$ are parity sequences, the composition $Q \circ P$ denotes the
parity sequence obtained by concatenating $P$ then $Q$: first apply $P$,
then apply $Q$ to the result.

\begin{theorem}[Composition rule]
\label{thm:composition}
Let $P$ and $Q$ be parity sequences with $o_P, e_P$ and $o_Q, e_Q$ odd and
even steps respectively.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:composition}
  \kpoly_{Q \circ P}(g,h)
  \;=\; g^{o_Q} \cdot \kpoly_P(g,h) \;+\; h^{e_P} \cdot \kpoly_Q(g,h).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $(\kappa^P_0, \ldots, \kappa^P_{o_P-1})$ and
$(\kappa^Q_0, \ldots, \kappa^Q_{o_Q-1})$ be the $\kappa$-tuples of
$P$ and $Q$ respectively.

In the concatenated sequence $Q \circ P$, the first $o_P$ odd steps come from
$P$ and the last $o_Q$ odd steps come from $Q$.  The $E$-steps of $P$ all
precede the $E$-steps of $Q$.

For the $j$-th odd step of $P$ ($0 \leq j < o_P$): the number of $E$-steps
preceding it in $Q \circ P$ equals $\kappa^P_j$ (no $E$-steps from $Q$ are
involved).

For the $j$-th odd step of $Q$ ($0 \leq j < o_Q$): the number of $E$-steps
preceding it in $Q \circ P$ is $e_P + \kappa^Q_j$ (all $e_P$ steps of $P$
plus the $\kappa^Q_j$ steps of $Q$ that precede it).

Therefore the structural polynomial of $Q \circ P$ is
\begin{align*}
  \kpoly_{Q\circ P}(g,h)
  &= \sum_{j=0}^{o_P-1} g^{(o_P + o_Q - 1) - j}\, h^{\kappa^P_j}
     + \sum_{j=0}^{o_Q-1} g^{o_Q - 1 - j}\, h^{e_P + \kappa^Q_j} \\
  &= g^{o_Q} \sum_{j=0}^{o_P-1} g^{o_P-1-j}\, h^{\kappa^P_j}
     + h^{e_P} \sum_{j=0}^{o_Q-1} g^{o_Q-1-j}\, h^{\kappa^Q_j} \\
  &= g^{o_Q}\cdot\kpoly_P(g,h) + h^{e_P}\cdot\kpoly_Q(g,h). \qedhere
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The two terms in~\eqref{eq:composition} have natural interpretations.
The factor $g^{o_Q}$ on $\kpoly_P$ accounts for the fact that after
$P$ has run, $Q$ applies $o_Q$ further odd multiplications, each of which
scales the additive accumulation from $P$ by $g$.  The factor $h^{e_P}$
on $\kpoly_Q$ accounts for the fact that $Q$'s own $E$-accumulation
exponents are displaced upward by the $e_P$ even divisions already consumed
by $P$.
\end{remark}

The composition rule tells us how structural polynomials combine, but it
leaves open a complementary question: given a fixed word $w$, which starting
values $a$ are compatible with it as a path, and which make it a cycle?
Section~\ref{sec:affine-paths} answers this by reading the path identity as
a congruence constraint on $a$.

\section{Affine Path Equations}
\label{sec:affine-paths}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The path identity~\eqref{eq:path} can be read in two directions.  Read
as a statement about a specific pair $(a,b)$, it asserts that starting
from $a$ and following word $w$ arrives at $b$.  Read as a constraint,
it characterises the full family of integer pairs $(a,b)$ for which $w$
is a valid path.

\subsection{The admissibility constraint}

Throughout this section we assume $\gcd(g,h) = 1$.

For a word $w$ with $o$ odd steps and $e$ even steps, the path identity is
\[
  h^e \cdot b \;=\; g^o \cdot a \;+\; q \cdot \kpoly_w(g,h).
\]
For $b$ to be a positive integer we need $h^e \mid g^o a + q\kpoly_w(g,h)$.
Since $\gcd(g^o, h^e) = 1$ when $\gcd(g,h)=1$, this is a congruence
condition on $a$ alone:
\[
  g^o \cdot a \;\equiv\; -q\kpoly_w(g,h) \pmod{h^e}.
\]
Because $\gcd(g,h)=1$ implies $g^o$ is invertible modulo $h^e$, this has
a unique solution $r \in \{0, 1, \ldots, h^e - 1\}$:
\[
  r \;\equiv\; -q\kpoly_w(g,h) \cdot (g^o)^{-1} \pmod{h^e}.
\]
We call $r$ the \emph{admissibility residue} of $w$.  (This use of
``admissibility'' is distinct from the orbit-level sense in the remark
after Definition~\ref{def:eprofile}: here it means integrality of each
E-step's output, not legality of the driving parity sequence.)

\subsection{The affine path family}

Every valid starting point $a$ for a path with word $w$ satisfies
$a \equiv r \pmod{h^e}$, so the family of integer pairs is parameterised
by $t \geq 0$:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:affine-path}
  a(t) \;=\; h^e \cdot t + r, \qquad
  b(t) \;=\; g^o \cdot t + b_0,
\end{equation}
where $b_0 = (g^o r + q\kpoly_w(g,h))/h^e$ is the image of $r$ under
$\Phi_w$.  Both $a(t)$ and $b(t)$ are affine functions of $t$; the path
$a \to b$ is the letter-driven trajectory prescribed by $w$.

\begin{remark}
The residue $r$ encodes the admissibility of the path: a starting value
$a$ is compatible with word $w$ if and only if $a \equiv r \pmod{h^e}$.
This is the modular shadow of the requirement that each prescribed E-step
is applied to an even value.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[$\St(2,1)$ at the Collatz basis]
For $w = \St(2,1) = \texttt{OEOEE}$ at $(g,h,q) = (3,2,1)$: $o=2$, $e=3$,
$\kpoly_w(3,2) = g + h = 5$, $h^e = 8$, $g^o = 9$.  Since $9 \equiv 1
\pmod{8}$, the admissibility residue is $r \equiv -5 \cdot 1 \equiv 3
\pmod{8}$.  The affine family is $a(t) = 8t + 3$, $b(t) = 9t + 4$,
giving the paths $3 \to 4$, $11 \to 13$, $19 \to 22$, $27 \to 31$, \ldots
\end{example}

\subsection{Intersection with cycles}

Setting $b = a$ in~\eqref{eq:affine-path} requires
\[
  h^e \cdot t + r \;=\; g^o \cdot t + b_0,
\]
hence $(h^e - g^o) \cdot t = b_0 - r$, i.e.\ $d \cdot t = b_0 - r$.
This has an integer solution in $t$ if and only if $d \mid (b_0 - r)$,
and when it does, the solution is unique:
\[
  t^* \;=\; \frac{b_0 - r}{d}, \qquad a^* \;=\; h^e \cdot t^* + r.
\]
Substituting $t^* = (b_0 - r)/d$ into $a^* = h^e t^* + r$ and using
$b_0 = (g^o r + q k)/h^e$:
\[
  d \cdot a^* = h^e b_0 - g^o r
              = (g^o r + qk) - g^o r
              = qk.
\]
The residue $r$ cancels exactly, giving the integer identity $d\cdot a^* = qk$
— the cycle element identity developed in Section~\ref{sec:cycle}.
The affine path family thus makes explicit what the cycle identity summarises:
a cycle exists when the path family and the diagonal $a = b$ intersect at a
non-negative integer $t^*$, and at that intersection the admissibility residue
drops out.

\begin{remark}
For path problems, $t$ ranges freely over $\ZZ_{\geq 0}$, giving an
infinite arithmetic progression of valid $(a,b)$ pairs.  For cycle
problems, $t$ is pinned to the single value $t^*$ (if it exists and is
a non-negative integer).  The transition from path to cycle is therefore
a transition from a free parameter to a determined one — the same
phenomenon seen in the cycle element identity, here made geometric.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[The $5x+1$ cycle at $a = 17$]
\label{ex:affine-5x1}
Consider the clause $w = \texttt{OEOEEEOEEE}$ at basis $(g,h,q) = (5,2,1)$:
$o = 3$, $e = 7$, $h^e = 128$, $g^o = 125$, $d = 3$.
The $\kappa$-tuple is $(0,1,4)$, giving $\kpoly_w(g,h) = g^2 + gh + h^4$
and $\kpoly_w(5,2) = 25 + 10 + 16 = 51$.

The admissibility residue is $r \equiv -51 \cdot (125)^{-1} \pmod{128}$.
Since $125 \equiv -3 \pmod{128}$ and $(-3)^{-1} \equiv 85 \pmod{128}$
(as $(-3)(85) = -255 \equiv 1 \pmod{128}$):
\[
  r \;\equiv\; -51 \cdot 85 \;=\; -4335 \;\equiv\; 17 \pmod{128}.
\]
The image of $r$ under $\Phi_w$ is $b_0 = (125 \cdot 17 + 51)/128 = 17$,
so the affine family is
\[
  a(t) = 128t + 17, \qquad b(t) = 125t + 17.
\]
Setting $a = b$: $3t = b_0 - r = 0$, giving $t^* = 0$ and $a^* = 17$.
The equation $3t = 0$ has exactly one non-negative integer solution,
so $a = 17$ is the unique element of this cycle.
\end{example}

\section{The Cycle Element Identity}
\label{sec:cycle}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Section~\ref{sec:affine-paths} derived the cycle element identity as a
geometric consequence: setting $a = b$ in the affine path family forces
$d \cdot a^* = qk$.  We now develop this identity as an algebraic object
in its own right, establishing the reduction theory and the successor
operation that generate all cycle representatives.

Setting $a = b$ in the path identity~\eqref{eq:path} gives the
\emph{cycle element identity}: for any fixed point $\Phi_w(a) = a$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:cycle-intro}
  (h^e - g^o)\cdot a \;=\; q \cdot \kpoly_w(g,h).
\end{equation}
Writing $d = h^e - g^o$ and $k = \kpoly_w(g,h)$, the \emph{natural solution}
$(a,q) = (k,d)$ is always an integer solution, so every abstract cycle word
is immediately realisable.  The interesting question is which cycles admit a
\emph{reduced} solution — one where $\gcd(a,q) = 1$ and in particular $q=1$.
We develop the reduction theory in four stages: the natural encoding
(Proposition~\ref{prop:natural-encoding}), the uniform factor lemma for
coprime bases (Lemma~\ref{lem:natural-factor}), the joint gcd for non-coprime
bases (Lemma~\ref{lem:cycle-gcd}), and the reduced identity and its
consequences (Theorems~\ref{thm:cycle}--\ref{thm:q-determined},
Propositions~\ref{prop:pointwise}--\ref{prop:ksucc}).

Throughout this section $\gcd$ denotes the positive greatest common divisor,
so $\gcd(k,|d|) > 0$ always, and the sign of $q_w = d/\gcd(k,|d|)$ matches
the sign of $d$.

\begin{proposition}[Every abstract cycle has a natural encoding]
\label{prop:natural-encoding}
Let $w$ be any parity sequence with $o \geq 1$ odd steps and $e$ even steps,
and let $(g,h)$ be any integer pair.  Setting $q = d = h^e - g^o$ and
$a = k = \kpoly_w(g,h)$, the pair $(a,q)$ satisfies $\Phi_w(a) = a$ at
basis $(g,h,q)$.  In particular, every abstract cycle word $w$ is realised
as a genuine integer cycle at every integer pair $(g,h)$ simply by choosing
$q = h^e - g^o$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The pair $(a,q) = (k,d)$ satisfies $d \cdot k = d \cdot k$ trivially,
hence $d \cdot a = q \cdot k$.  Setting $b = a$ in the path
identity~\eqref{eq:path} shows this is equivalent to $\Phi_w(a) = a$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Since the natural solution exists for every $(g,h)$ and every word $w$, the
existence of an integer cycle is not in itself remarkable — it is guaranteed
by construction once $q = d$.  What matters is \emph{reduction}: when
$\gcd(g,h)=1$ the factor $f = \gcd(k,|d|)$ is uniform across the whole
cycle (Lemma~\ref{lem:natural-factor} below), and dividing through gives
the reduced solution $a_w = k/f$, $q_w = d/f$ with $\gcd(a_w,|q_w|)=1$.
The mythical cycles are the reduced cycles at the standard Collatz basis
$(g,h,q) = (3,2,1)$ with $o > 1$: these would require $|d| = |2^e - 3^o|$
to divide $\kpoly_w(3,2)$, and no such cycle is known to exist.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Natural cycles carry a uniform factor]
\label{lem:natural-factor}
Let $\gcd(g,h) = 1$, let $w$ be a parity sequence with $o \geq 1$, and let
$w_0, w_1, \ldots, w_{n-1}$ be the cyclic rotations of $w$ with structural
values $k_i = \kpoly_{w_i}(g,h)$.  Set $d = h^e - g^o$ and
$f = \gcd(k_0, |d|)$.  Then $f \mid k_i$ for every $i$, and consequently
$f \mid \gcd(k_0, k_1, \ldots, k_{n-1}, |d|)$.  In particular, the
reduced solution $a_w = k_0/f$, $q_w = d/f$ is a valid integer cycle.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We show $f \mid k_i$ is preserved under each rotation step.  By hypothesis
$f \mid k_0$ and $f \mid |d|$.

\emph{O-leading step} ($w_i$ begins with O): the successor value satisfies
$k_{i+1} = g k_i + d$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:ksucc}).  Since $f \mid k_i$
and $f \mid d$, we have $f \mid g k_i + d = k_{i+1}$.

\emph{E-leading step} ($w_i$ begins with E): the successor value satisfies
$k_{i+1} = k_i / h$.  Since $\gcd(g,h) = 1$, we have $\gcd(h, d) =
\gcd(h, h^e - g^o) = \gcd(h, g^o) = 1$, so $\gcd(f, h) = 1$.  Together
with $f \mid k_i$ and $h \mid k_i$ (required for the E-step to yield an
integer), $\gcd(f,h) = 1$ gives $f \mid k_i/h = k_{i+1}$.

Since $f$ divides every $k_i$ and $|d|$, the reduced pair
$(k_0/f,\, d/f)$ satisfies $d \cdot (k_0/f) = (d/f) \cdot k_0$,
confirming it is a valid integer cycle.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This lemma explains the role of $\gcd(g,h) = 1$ in the reduction argument.
It is not that reduced cycles fail to exist when $\gcd(g,h) > 1$ — they
may still exist — but the propagation argument above breaks down at the
E-leading step: $\gcd(f,h) = 1$ need not hold when $h$ and $d$ share a
common factor, so $f \mid k_i$ does not automatically imply $f \mid k_i/h$.
When $\gcd(g,h) = 1$, every natural cycle carries the uniform factor
$f = \gcd(k_0, |d|)$, and dividing through always produces a reduced cycle.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Cycle gcd under non-coprime bases]
\label{lem:cycle-gcd}
Let $w = w_0 w_1 \cdots w_{n-1}$ be a parity sequence with $o$ odd steps,
$e$ even steps, $d = h^e - g^o$, and let $r = \gcd(g,h)$.  If $r > 1$
then $r \mid \kpoly_{w_i}(g,h)$ for every rotation $w_i$ of $w$, and
$r \mid d$.  Consequently $\gcd(\kpoly_{w_i}(g,h),\, d)$ is divisible by
$r$ for each $i$, and in general
\[
  \gcd(\kpoly_{w_i}(g,h),\, d) \;\neq\; \gcd(\kpoly_{w_j}(g,h),\, d)
\]
for distinct rotations $i \neq j$.  The primitive solution to the cycle
system therefore requires the \emph{joint} gcd
\[
  \delta_w \;:=\; \gcd\!\bigl(\kpoly_{w_0}(g,h),\, \kpoly_{w_1}(g,h),\, \ldots,\,
                              \kpoly_{w_{n-1}}(g,h),\, |d|\bigr),
\]
not the single-rotation gcd $\gcd(\kpoly_{w_i}(g,h), d)$.  When
$\gcd(g,h) = 1$, the single-rotation gcd suffices: $\delta_w =
\gcd(\kpoly_w(g,h),\, |d|)$ for any rotation $w$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every monomial $g^{o-1-j}h^{\kappa_j}$ of $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ has positive
total degree in $g$ and $h$ (since $o \geq 1$), so $r \mid \kpoly_{w_i}$
for every rotation $w_i$.  Since $r \mid g^o$ and $r \mid h^e$, we have
$r \mid d$.  Hence $r$ divides every individual $\gcd(\kpoly_{w_i}, d)$,
and $\gcd(\kpoly_{w_i}, d)$ may differ from $\gcd(\kpoly_{w_j}, d)$ for
$i \neq j$ because the spurious factor $r$ is distributed unevenly across
rotation values.  The joint gcd $\delta_w$ absorbs only the common content
of all rotations and is therefore the correct divisor.  When $\gcd(g,h)=1$,
the rotation recurrence (Proposition~\ref{prop:ksucc}) acts by
multiplication by $g$ or $h^{-1}$ modulo $d$, so all $\kpoly_{w_i}$ lie
in the same $\gcd(\cdot,d)$-class, and the single-rotation formula suffices.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The failure of the single-rotation formula when $\gcd(g,h) > 1$ is
illustrated concretely by $w = \texttt{OEOEEOEE}$ at $(g,h) = (4,2)$,
where $r = 2$.  Here $d = 2^5 - 4^3 = -32$, and the eight structural
values $\kpoly_{w_i}(4,2)$ are $32, 96, 48, 160, 80, 40, 128, 64$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Per-rotation reduction} (using $\gcd(k_i, d)$ for each $i$
separately) gives inconsistent results:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcrrr}
\hline
Rotation & $k_i$ & $\gcd(k_i,|d|)$ & $a_i = k_i/\gcd$ & $q_i = d/\gcd$ \\
\hline
\texttt{OEOEEOEE} & 32 & 32 & 1 & $-1$ \\
\texttt{EOEEOEEO} & 96 & 32 & 3 & $-1$ \\
\texttt{OEEOEEOE} & 48 & 16 & 3 & $-2$ \\
\texttt{EEOEEOEO} & 160 & 32 & 5 & $-1$ \\
\texttt{EOEEOEOE} & 80 & 16 & 5 & $-2$ \\
\texttt{OEEOEOEE} & 40 &  8 & 5 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EEOEOEEO} & 128 & 32 & 4 & $-1$ \\
\texttt{EOEOEEOE} & 64 & 32 & 2 & $-1$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The $q_i$ values are inconsistent ($-1$, $-2$, $-4$) and the $a_i$
values repeat (the value $5$ appears three times, $3$ twice), so per-rotation
reduction does not yield a valid $8$-cycle with distinct elements.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Joint-gcd reduction} (using $\delta_w = \gcd(32,96,48,160,80,40,128,64,|d|) = 8$
for all rotations) gives consistent results:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcrrr}
\hline
Rotation & $k_i$ & $\delta_w$ & $a_i = k_i/\delta_w$ & $q_i = d/\delta_w$ \\
\hline
\texttt{OEOEEOEE} & 32 & 8 & 4 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EOEEOEEO} & 96 & 8 & 12 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{OEEOEEOE} & 48 & 8 & 6 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EEOEEOEO} & 160 & 8 & 20 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EOEEOEOE} & 80 & 8 & 10 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{OEEOEOEE} & 40 & 8 & 5 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EEOEOEEO} & 128 & 8 & 16 & $-4$ \\
\texttt{EOEOEEOE} & 64 & 8 & 8 & $-4$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The $q_i$ values are now uniformly $-4$ and the eight $a_i$ values
$4, 12, 6, 20, 10, 5, 16, 8$ are all distinct, giving a valid $8$-cycle.

\smallskip
Note that in this example the cycle steps are driven by the parity sequence
directly, not by the residue of the current element modulo $h$.  When
$\gcd(g,h) > 1$, the parity of $a_i$ modulo $h$ is not well-defined as a
basis for deciding the next step, so the letter-driven interpretation
(where $w$ prescribes which steps are $O$-steps and which are $E$-steps)
is the only coherent one.

\smallskip
(For comparison, the same word at $(g,h)=(3,2)$ has $d=5$, $k=23$,
$\gcd(k,|d|)=1$, giving $q_w=5$, $a_w=23$ — the standard Collatz cycle
$[23, 74, 37, 116, 58, 29, 92, 46]$.)
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Reduced cycle element identity]
\label{thm:cycle}
Let $w$ be a parity sequence with $o$ odd steps, $e$ even steps,
$d = h^e - g^o$, and $k = \kpoly_w(g,h)$.  Define
\[
  \delta \;:=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \gcd(k,\,|d|) & \text{if } \gcd(g,h) = 1, \\[4pt]
    \gcd(k_0,\,k_1,\,\ldots,\,k_{n-1},\,|d|) & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
\]
where $k_i = \kpoly_{w_i}(g,h)$ denotes the structural value of the $i$-th
rotation of $w$.  Then the \emph{reduced solution}
to~\eqref{eq:cycle-intro} (with $\gcd(a_w,|q_w|)=1$) is
\[
  a_w \;=\; \frac{k}{\delta}, \qquad q_w \;=\; \frac{d}{\delta},
\]
Here $a_w > 0$ and $q_w$ has the sign of $d$.  Every other solution with
$a > 0$ is a positive-integer multiple of $(a_w, q_w)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The natural solution $(k,d)$ satisfies~\eqref{eq:cycle-intro} by
inspection.  Dividing both sides by $\delta$ gives the reduced solution
$(k/\delta,\, d/\delta)$ with $\gcd(k/\delta,\, |d/\delta|) = 1$.  Any
solution $(a,q)$ with $\gcd(a,|q|)=s>1$ yields a smaller solution $(a/s,
q/s)$, so the reduced solution is the unique one with $\gcd(a,|q|)=1$ and
$a > 0$.  When $\gcd(g,h)=1$, Lemma~\ref{lem:natural-factor} guarantees
that $\delta = \gcd(k,|d|)$ divides every rotation value, so the
single-rotation formula is valid.  When $\gcd(g,h) > 1$, the joint gcd is
required by Lemma~\ref{lem:cycle-gcd}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Cycle determines $q$]
\label{thm:q-determined}
Let $(g,h)$ be an integer pair with $\gcd(g,h)=1$, and let $w$ be a parity
sequence with $o$ odd steps, $e$ even steps, $d = h^e - g^o$, and
$k = \kpoly_w(g,h)$.  Given $w$ and $(g,h)$, the additive term $q$ is not a
free parameter: the reduced value
\[
  q_w \;=\; \frac{d}{\gcd(k,\,|d|)}
\]
is the unique reduced $q$ for which the integer fixed point
$a_w = k/\gcd(k,|d|) > 0$ exists.  In particular:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $q_w$ has the sign of $d = h^e - g^o$.
  \item $q_w = 1$ if and only if $|d| \mid \kpoly_w(g,h)$.
  \item Every other solution with $a > 0$ uses $q = m q_w$ for some
        $m \in \ZZ_{>0}$, with corresponding cycle element $m a_w$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:cycle}, every solution $(a,q)$ with $a > 0$ to
$d \cdot a = q \cdot k$ is a positive-integer multiple of the reduced
solution $(a_w, q_w)$.  Hence $q$ is constrained to $\{m q_w : m \in
\ZZ_{>0}\}$; the three stated properties follow directly from the
definition of $q_w = d/\gcd(k,|d|)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Polynomial vs.\ pointwise divisibility]
\label{prop:pointwise}
Polynomial non-divisibility of $h^e - g^o$ into $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ in
$\ZZ[g,h]$ does \emph{not} imply pointwise non-divisibility at any particular
integer pair $(g,h)$.  Consequently, no argument based solely on polynomial
identities can rule out the existence of integer cycles at a specific basis.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The $5x+1$ cycle at $a = 17$ with clause $w = \texttt{OEOEEEOEEE}$ is a concrete
witness (Example~\ref{ex:5x1}): the polynomial $h^7 - g^3$ does not divide
$\kpoly_w(g,h) = g^2 + gh + h^4$ in $\ZZ[g,h]$, yet at $(g,h) = (5,2)$
one has $2^7 - 5^3 = 3$ and $\kpoly_w(5,2) = 51 = 3 \times 17$, so $3
\mid 51$.  The cycle exists because of the arithmetic coincidence $2^7 - 5^3
= 3$, which is invisible at the polynomial level.  Any argument ruling out
Collatz cycles must therefore work at the level of integer evaluations.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Successor on $\kpoly_w$ around a cycle]
\label{prop:ksucc}
Let $(g,h,q)$ be a basis with $\gcd(g,h) = 1$, and let
$w = w_0 w_1 \cdots w_{n-1}$ be a cyclic parity sequence with $o$ odd
steps and $e$ even steps in total, and let $w' = w_1 \cdots w_{n-1} w_0$
be the rotation by one step.  Then, working in $\ZZ/(h^e-g^o)\ZZ$,
\[
  \kpoly_{w'}(g,h)
  \;\equiv\;
  \begin{cases}
    g\cdot\kpoly_w(g,h) & \text{if } \kpoly_w(1,0) = 1\ (\text{$w$ begins with an $O$-step}), \\[4pt]
    h^{-1}\cdot\kpoly_w(g,h) & \text{if } \kpoly_w(1,0) = 0\ (\text{$w$ begins with an $E$-step}).
  \end{cases}
\]
In the $O$-step case, the unreduced product $g\cdot\kpoly_w(g,h)$ promotes
the leading monomial $g^{o-1}\mapsto g^o$, and the natural recurrence
\[
  \kpoly_{w'}(g,h) \;=\; g\cdot\kpoly_w(g,h) + (h^e - g^o)
\]
replaces this $g^o$ by $h^e$ — exactly the substitution $g^o\equiv h^e
\pmod{h^e-g^o}$ — keeping the degree at $o-1$ in $g$.  In the $E$-step
case, $h^{-1}$ exists in $\ZZ/(h^e-g^o)\ZZ$ because $\gcd(g,h)=1$ implies
$\gcd(h,\,h^e-g^o)=1$.  The modulus $h^e - g^o$ is the cycle denominator
from Theorem~\ref{thm:cycle}, so this operation is only natural in the
context of a fixed cycle $(o,e)$.
As a consequence, if $w$ is primitive (not a proper power of a shorter word),
the $n$ rotations of $w$ produce at most $n$ distinct values of
$\kpoly_{w'}(g,h)$ in $\ZZ/(h^e-g^o)\ZZ$.
For a non-primitive $w$ of period $p \mid n$, there are at most $p$ distinct values.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
When $d \mid \kpoly_w$, the reduced solution has $q_w = 1$, recovering the
standard Collatz setting.
A worked example ($w = OEEOEOEE$, $q_w = 5$, $a_w = 29$) appears in
Section~\ref{sec:grand-cycle}.
\end{remark}

The reduction theory fixes the reduced solution $(a_w, q_w)$ uniquely once
$(w, g, h)$ are given.  Section~\ref{sec:cycle-flavours} steps back and
classifies the full family of normalisations of the solution pair $(a, q)$.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Cycle Flavours}
\label{sec:cycle-flavours}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The preceding section established that once $(w, g, h)$ are fixed, the
reduced solution $(a_w, q_w)$ is unique.  But the equation $d \cdot a = q \cdot k$
admits infinitely many integer solutions related by overall scaling and sign,
and different conventions on the signs and integrality of $a$ and $q$
arise naturally in different contexts.  We call these \emph{cycle flavours}.
They are not distinct cycles: they are the same underlying algebraic object
viewed through different choices of normalisation.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{lllc}
\hline
\textbf{Flavour} & \textbf{$a$} & \textbf{$q$} & \textbf{$\gcd(|a|,|q|)$} \\
\hline
Natural  & $k > 0$ & $d$ (any sign) & $\geq 1$ \\
Reduced  & $k/\gcd(k,|d|) > 0$ & $d/\gcd(k,|d|)$ (any sign) & $1$ \\
Negative & $< 0$ & $> 0$ & $1$ \\
Rational & $k/d$ (any sign) & $1$ & $-$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each flavour is an independently defined normalisation of the solutions
to $d \cdot a = q \cdot k$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Natural $\leftrightarrow$ scaled:} multiplying both $a$
    and $q$ by any $m \in \ZZ_{>0}$ gives another valid solution.  The
    natural solution $(k, d)$ is the representative with $a = k$.

  \item \textbf{Reduced:} dividing the natural solution by
    $\gcd(k,|d|)$ gives $a = k/\gcd(k,|d|) > 0$ and
    $q = d/\gcd(k,|d|)$, with $q$ inheriting the sign of $d$ and
    $\gcd(|a|,|q|) = 1$.  This is the normalisation used throughout
    the paper.

  \item \textbf{Negative:} an independently defined sign-reassignment
    normalisation: $a < 0$, $q > 0$, $\gcd(|a|,|q|) = 1$.  No factor
    elimination is performed; the sign of the primitive solution is
    assigned so that $q > 0$ rather than so that $a > 0$.

  \item \textbf{Rational:} setting $q = 1$ gives $a = k/d \in \mathbb{Q}$.
    This is an integer exactly when $d \mid k$, i.e.\ when the reduced
    cycle has $q_w = 1$.  The sign of $a$ matches the sign of $d$.
\end{itemize}

The preferred flavour in this paper is \emph{reduced} with $a > 0$.
The mythical Collatz cycles are the reduced cycles at $(g,h,q) = (3,2,1)$
with $o > 1$: they sit at the intersection of the reduced and rational
flavours, being the primitive solutions ($\gcd(a,q)=1$, $a > 0$) for
which additionally $q = 1$ and $a \in \ZZ$.

The flavour classification applies to any fixed specialisation $(g,h)$.
Section~\ref{sec:pvalue} steps back and asks a different question: how many
ways can a parity sequence be represented, independently of any basis?

\begin{definition}[Orbit parity sequence $\phi_w$]
\label{def:phi}
Let $w$ be a parity sequence with $o$ odd steps, $e$ even steps, and
$\kpoly_w(3,2) = k$.  Set $q = 2^e - 3^o$ and define the sequence
$a_0 = k,\; a_{i+1} = \Phi_{\text{step}}(a_i)$ under the $(3,2,q)$ Collatz
map.  The \emph{orbit parity sequence} of $w$ is the parity sequence
\[
  \phi_w \;:=\; (s_0, s_1, \ldots, s_{n-1}), \qquad
  s_i = \begin{cases} O & \text{if } a_i \text{ is odd}, \\ E & \text{if } a_i \text{ is even}. \end{cases}
\]
\end{definition}

Every parity sequence $w$ of length $n$ with $o$ odd steps lives simultaneously
in five equivalent representations.  The canonical integer encoding
$(\text{bits}, p_w)$ sits at the centre; four structural representations ---
$\sigma_w(u,v)$, $\kpoly_w(g,h)$, $\kpoly_w(3,2)$, and $\phi_w$
(Definition~\ref{def:phi}) --- surround it.  The diagram below serves as a

\subsection{The $p$-value (edges B, C; centre node)}

Since $\kpoly_w$ is independent of trailing $E$-steps (Remark~\ref{rem:trailing}),
it does not uniquely identify a parity sequence.  A simple specialisation
recovers a canonical integer encoding, and a sentinel bit resolves the
trailing-$E$ ambiguity.

\begin{proposition}[Bit-encoding of parity sequences]
\label{prop:bits}
Let $w = b_0 b_1 \cdots b_{n-1}$ be a parity sequence of length $n$, where
$b_i = 1$ if the $i$-th step is an $O$-step and $b_i = 0$ if it is an
$E$-step.  Then
\[
  N_w \;:=\; 2^{o-1}\cdot\kpoly_w\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2},\, 2\right)
  \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} b_i\, 2^i,
\]
the non-negative integer whose binary representation encodes $w$ directly
(LSB-first, $O = 1$, $E = 0$).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The $j$-th term of $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ is $g^{o-1-j} h^{\kappa_j}$.  Evaluating
at $g = 1/2$, $h = 2$ and multiplying by $2^{o-1}$:
\[
  2^{o-1} \cdot \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{o-1-j} \cdot 2^{\kappa_j}
  = 2^{j + \kappa_j}.
\]
Since $\kappa_j$ counts $E$-steps before the $j$-th $O$-step and $j$ counts
$O$-steps before it, $j + \kappa_j = p_j$ is the position of the $j$-th
$O$-step, i.e.\ the index $i$ with $b_i = 1$.  Summing over $j$ gives
$\sum_j 2^{p_j} = \sum_i b_i 2^i = N_w$.
\end{proof}

Sequences differing only in trailing $E$-steps (i.e.\ trailing $b_i = 0$)
share the same $N_w$.  To resolve this ambiguity we append a sentinel bit
at position $n$.

\begin{definition}[$p$-value]
\label{def:pvalue}
The \emph{$p$-value} of a parity sequence $w = b_0 b_1 \cdots b_{n-1}$ is
\[
  p_w \;:=\; \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} b_i\, 2^i \;+\; 2^n
  \;=\; 2^{o-1}\cdot\kpoly_w\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2},2\right) + 2^n.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[$p$-value uniqueness]
\label{thm:pvalue}
The map $w \mapsto p_w$ is a bijection from the set of all finite parity
sequences over $\{O,E\}$ (including the empty sequence) to the set of
integers $\geq 1$.  The empty sequence has $p_\varepsilon = 2^0 = 1$.
For non-empty $w$, $w$ is uniquely recovered from $p_w$ by writing $p_w$
in binary: the highest set bit (at position $n$) records the length, and
bits $0, \ldots, n-1$ give the sequence with $1 = O$ and $0 = E$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The empty sequence has $n = 0$, $N_\varepsilon = 0$, and $p_\varepsilon = 0 + 2^0 = 1$.
For non-empty $w$, given $p_w \geq 2$, let $n = \lfloor \log_2 p_w \rfloor \geq 1$
(the position of the highest set bit).  Then bits $0, \ldots, n-1$ of $p_w$
coincide with $N_w$ and uniquely determine the positions of all $O$-steps,
hence the full sequence $w$.  The map is therefore injective.  Surjectivity
onto integers $\geq 1$ follows since every positive integer has a unique
highest set bit at some position $n \geq 0$: $n = 0$ gives the empty
sequence, and $n \geq 1$ gives the parity sequence whose $i$-th step is $O$
iff bit $i$ of $p_w$ is $1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Successor as cyclic bit rotation]
The successor $w \mapsto w'$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:ksucc}) acts on the
$p$-value as a \emph{right} rotation of the lower $n$ bits.  Reading the
data bits in the conventional MSB-first order as $b_{n-1} b_{n-2} \cdots b_1 b_0$,
the right rotation moves the rightmost bit $b_0$ to the leftmost data position:
\[
  p_{w'} \;=\; 2^n + b_0\, 2^{n-1} + b_{n-1}\, 2^{n-2} + \cdots + b_1.
\]
The sentinel bit at position $n$ is unchanged, reflecting the fact that all
cycle representatives have the same length $n$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The $\sigma$-polynomial (edges A, D)}

\subsubsection*{Change of variables}

The $\sigma$-polynomial arises from $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ by the substitution
$g = u/v$, $h = v$, followed by clearing the denominator $v^{o-1}$:
$\sigma_w(u,v) = \kpoly_w(u/v,\,v)\cdot v^{o-1} \in \ZZ[u,v]$.
The inverse substitution $u = gh$, $v = h$ recovers
$\kpoly_w(g,h) = \sigma_w(gh,h)/h^{o-1}$.

\begin{definition}[$\sigma$-polynomial]
\label{def:sigma}
For a parity sequence $w$ of length $n$ with $o$ odd steps and $O$-step
positions $p_0 < \cdots < p_{o-1}$, define
\[
  \sigma_w(u,v) \;:=\; \kpoly_w\!\left(\tfrac{u}{v},\, v\right) \cdot v^{o-1}
  \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{o-1} u^{o-1-j}\, v^{p_j}
  \;\in\; \ZZ[u,v].
\]
\end{definition}

The exponent of $u$ in the $j$-th term counts the $O$-steps \emph{after}
position $j$; the exponent of $v$ is the bit-position of the $j$-th $O$-step.
Specialising to $u = 1$, $v = 2$ recovers the bit-encoding: $N_w = \sigma_w(1,2)$,
and consequently $p_w = \sigma_w(1,2) + 2^n$ (edge A inverse).

\subsubsection*{The successor operation on $\sigma$}

Each step of the Collatz map acts on $\sigma_w(u,v)$ by an elegant polynomial
operation~\cite{paper14}.  Define
\[
  \gamma_w \;:=\; \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } p_0 = 0 \text{ (i.e.\ $w$ begins with an $O$-step)}, \\
                                0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
\tag{$\gamma$}\label{def:gamma}
\]
Equivalently, $\gamma_w = 1$ if and only if the leading term $u^{o-1} v^{p_0}$
of $\sigma_w$ has $v$-exponent zero.  Define
\[
  \operatorname{succ}_\sigma(\sigma_w(u,v))
  \;:=\; u^{\gamma_w} \cdot \sigma_w(u,v) \cdot v^{-1}.
\]
Then $\operatorname{succ}_\sigma(\sigma_w(u,v)) = \sigma_{w'}(u,v)$, where $w'$
is the parity sequence obtained by rotating $w$ left by one step (moving the
first bit to the last position).

\begin{remark}
Adopting the convention $0^0 = 1$, we have $\gamma_w = \sigma_w(1, 0)$:
each term $1^{o-1-j} \cdot 0^{p_j}$ vanishes unless $p_j = 0$, which
occurs precisely when $j = 0$ and $w$ begins with an $O$-step.
\end{remark}

The successor formula is valid in any ring where $u^o = 1$ and $v^n = 1$:
the cycle closes after $n$ steps since $v^{-n} = 1$, returning to $\sigma_w$.
Complex roots of unity are one natural realisation of these constraints, but
the algebraic structure depends only on these two relations.

\subsubsection*{Evaluation at roots of unity}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:sigma-roots}
Let $\omega_o = e^{2\pi i/o}$ and $\omega_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$ be primitive
$o$-th and $n$-th roots of unity respectively.  The evaluation
\[
  \sigma_w(\omega_o,\, \omega_n)
  \;=\; \sum_{j=0}^{o-1} \omega_o^{o-1-j}\, \omega_n^{p_j}
\]
expresses the parity sequence as a linear combination of $n$-th roots of
unity, weighted by powers of $\omega_o$.  Each step of the cycle acts as
\[
  \operatorname{succ}_\sigma(\sigma_{w_k}(\omega_o, \omega_n))
  \;=\; \omega_o^{\gamma_{w_k}} \cdot \sigma_{w_k}(\omega_o, \omega_n) \cdot \omega_n^{-1},
\]
a multiplication by $\omega_o^{\gamma_{w_k}}$ (recording whether step $k$ is
an $O$-step) composed with a rotation $\omega_n^{-1}$ of the phase.
Since $\omega_o^o = 1$ and $\omega_n^n = 1$, the cycle closes
after exactly $n$ steps.  See~\cite{paper14} for the full development.
\end{proposition}

\section{Examples}
\label{sec:examples}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{example}[The $5x+1$ cycle at $17$]
\label{ex:5x1}
Under the Syracuse $5x+1$ map (basis $g = 5$, $h = 2$, $q = 1$), the odd
integer $17$ is a periodic point.  Its orbit is
\[
  17 \;\xrightarrow{O}\; 86 \;\xrightarrow{E}\; 43
     \;\xrightarrow{O}\; 216 \;\xrightarrow{E^3}\; 27
     \;\xrightarrow{O}\; 136 \;\xrightarrow{E^3}\; 17.
\]
The clause is $w = \texttt{OEOEEEOEEE}$, with $o = 3$, $e = 7$.

The $\kappa$-tuple is $\kappa_0 = 0$, $\kappa_1 = 1$, $\kappa_2 = 4$
(the three $O$-steps are preceded by $0$, $1$, and $4$ even steps
respectively).  Note that $(\kappa_0, \kappa_1, \kappa_2) = (0,1,4) \neq
(0,1,2)$, so $w$ is \emph{not} a Steiner word and the geometric polynomial
theorem does not apply directly.  The structural polynomial is
\[
  \kpoly_w(g,h) = g^2 h^0 + g^1 h^1 + g^0 h^4 = g^2 + gh + h^4.
\]
Evaluating at $(g,h) = (5,2)$:
\[
  \kpoly_w(5,2) = 25 + 10 + 16 = 51,
  \qquad
  h^e - g^o = 2^7 - 5^3 = 128 - 125 = 3.
\]
By Theorem~\ref{thm:cycle}, $d \cdot a = q \cdot k$ gives
\[
  3 \cdot a = 1 \cdot 51 = 51, \qquad a = 17. \quad \checkmark
\]
The near-miss $h^e - g^o = 3$ is what makes this cycle arithmetically possible:
$2^7 - 5^3 = 3$ is a specific arithmetic accident with no analogue for the
Collatz basis $(g,h) = (3,2)$.  This also illustrates Proposition~\ref{prop:pointwise}:
the polynomial $h^7 - g^3$ does not divide $g^2 + gh + h^4$ in $\ZZ[g,h]$,
yet divisibility holds at $(5,2)$ because of this accident.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[The $g = h^2 - 1$ family]
\label{ex:gh2}
When divisibility \emph{does} hold as a polynomial identity, it explains cycles
across an entire family of bases at once.  Consider $\St(1,1)^3$ (three copies
of $OEE$), with $o = 3$, $e = 6$.  By the composition rule:
\begin{align*}
  \kpoly_{\St(1,1)^2}(g,h) &= g + h^2, \\
  \kpoly_{\St(1,1)^3}(g,h) &= g^2 + gh^2 + h^4.
\end{align*}
On the locus $g = h^2 - 1$ one checks that $(g^2 + gh^2 + h^4) - (h^6 - g^3)
= (g - h^2 + 1)(g^2 + gh^2 + h^4)$ vanishes identically on the locus $g = h^2-1$
(since $g - h^2 + 1 = 0$ there), so
$\kpoly_{\St(1,1)^3} = h^6 - g^3$ and the cycle element
$a = q\cdot\kpoly/(h^6-g^3) = q$ for every such basis.  At $q=1$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $h=2$, $g=3$: orbit $1\xrightarrow{O}4\xrightarrow{EE}1$, three times.
  \item $h=3$, $g=8$: orbit $1\xrightarrow{O}9\xrightarrow{EE}1$, three times.
\end{itemize}
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Non-Collatz parity sequence]
\label{ex:noncollatz}
This example relaxes the requirement that the $\kappa$-tuple be strictly
increasing.  In the integer domain this corresponds to relaxing the rule
that $gx+q$ is applied only to odd integers; in both settings the result
is an ``illegal'' cycle that does not arise in standard Collatz dynamics.

Consider the parity sequence $w = OEEOOEEE$, with $o = 3$, $e = 5$.
The $\kappa$-tuple is $\kappa_0 = 0$, $\kappa_1 = 2$, $\kappa_2 = 2$:
both the second and third $O$-steps are preceded by exactly 2 even steps.
The structural polynomial is
\[
  \kpoly_{OEEOOEEE}(g,h)
    = g^{2}h^{0} + g^{1}h^{2} + g^{0}h^{2}
    = g^2 + h^2(g + 1).
\]

This $w$ is \emph{not} a valid standard Collatz parity sequence.
The equal values $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 2$ mean that two consecutive $O$-steps
occur without any intervening $E$-step---the subsequence $OO$ appears.
Under standard Collatz, an $O$-step must be applied to an odd integer, so
two consecutive $O$-steps are impossible: after $3n+1$ the result is even
and must be halved before another $O$-step can occur.  Equivalently, valid
Collatz sequences require $\kappa_0 < \kappa_1 < \cdots < \kappa_{o-1}$
(strictly increasing).

If one relaxes this single condition---allowing $\kappa_j \le \kappa_{j+1}$---and
correspondingly drives the $3x+1$ and $x/2$ operations by the letters of $w$
rather than the parity of the current element, then cycles can exist.  At
$(g,h) = (3,2)$ we get $\kpoly_w(3,2) = 3^2 + 2^2(3+1) = 9 + 16 = 25$ and
$h^e - g^o = 2^5 - 3^3 = 5$, so Theorem~\ref{thm:cycle} yields cycle element
$a = 25/5 = 5$.  The orbit from $a = 5$ is
\[
  5 \xrightarrow{O} 16 \xrightarrow{E} 8 \xrightarrow{E} 4
    \xrightarrow{O} 13 \xrightarrow{O} 40 \xrightarrow{E} 20
    \xrightarrow{E} 10 \xrightarrow{E} 5,
\]
which does return to $5$.  The single illegal step is $4 \xrightarrow{O} 13$:
applying $3x+1$ to the even integer $4$, forced by the repeated value
$\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 2$.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
The sequence $OEEOOEEE$ above has $p$-value $N_w + 2^8 = 25 + 256 = 281$:
the integer $281$ encodes the sequence directly in binary (with sentinel bit
at position $8$), and the glitched orbit was constructed by reading the bits
of $281 - 256 = 25$ as a parity sequence.
\end{remark}

The examples above illustrate the theory across a range of bases and word
types.  Section~\ref{sec:abstract-cycle} reflects on what this framework,
taken as a whole, reveals — and what it cannot.

\section{What the Abstract Path Tells Us — and What It Does Not}
\label{sec:abstract-cycle}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The preceding sections have established that a parity sequence $w$ admits
many equivalent representations: as the orbit parity sequence $\phi_w$,
as a binary integer $p_w$, as the $\sigma$-polynomial $\sigma_w(u,v)$,
as the structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ over an arbitrary basis,
and as the integer $\kpoly_w(3,2)$.  When $w$ is a cycle word, it
carries additionally the cycle flavour table of Section~\ref{sec:cycle-flavours}.
Each of these representations illuminates a different facet of the same
object, as the examples of Section~\ref{sec:examples} illustrate concretely:
the $5x+1$ cycle at $17$ (Example~\ref{ex:5x1}) and the $g = h^2 - 1$ family
(Example~\ref{ex:gh2}) both arise as specialisations of a single abstract
parity sequence to different bases.

Ironically, one of the least revealing representations of a cycle is the
one most commonly encountered in the literature: a cycle expressed as a
list of integers in a specific basis $(g,h,q)$.  When $k = \kpoly_w(g,h)$
and $d = h^e - g^o$ are collapsed into a single ratio $a = qk/d$, most
of the algebraic structure disappears.  The cycle element $a$ is just a
number.  The richness encoded in $\kpoly_w$ as a polynomial in $g$ and
$h$ — its monomial structure, the $\kappa$-tuple, the geometric sum form
for Steiner words — is invisible.  That the structure survives at all,
and that $a$ is an integer, is one of the small mysteries of mathematics;
but the \emph{why} is not apparent from the integer alone.

By contrast, the abstract representations make structure explicit.
The $\kappa$-tuple encodes exactly how E-steps are distributed before each
O-step.  The structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ makes the dependence
on the basis parameters transparent: one can see immediately how the cycle
element scales as $g$ or $h$ varies, and how cycles at different bases
relate to one another.  The $\sigma$-polynomial connects the combinatorial
structure of $w$ to geometry: the monomials are points in a lattice, and
the successor operation is a rotation.  The $p$-value gives a canonical
integer encoding that is independent of any basis.

\subsection*{All integer Collatz-like cycles are mere encodings of an abstract cycle}

Once a parity sequence $w$ and an encoding basis $(g,h)$ are chosen,
everything else is determined.  The cycle element $a_w = \kpoly_w(g,h) /
\gcd(\kpoly_w(g,h), |d|)$ and the additive parameter $q_w = d /
\gcd(\kpoly_w(g,h), |d|)$ follow with no further freedom.  There are no
free integer cycles: every integer cycle in any basis is an encoding of
some abstract parity sequence $w$, with the parameters $(a,q)$ fixed by
$(w, g, h)$ through the reduced cycle element identity.  The space of
abstract parity sequences is therefore in bijection with the space of
reduced integer cycles across all bases.

This perspective also reveals shared structure across different bases.
Two bases $(g_1,h_1,q_1)$ and $(g_2,h_2,q_2)$ that yield the same
parity sequence $w$ as a cycle are encoding the same abstract object.
The $g = h^2 - 1$ family (Example~\ref{ex:gh2}) makes this vivid: the
bases $(g,h) = (3,2)$ and $(g,h) = (8,3)$ share the clause $\St(1,1)^3$
as a cycle word, differing only in the specialisation of the structural
polynomial.  More generally, the structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$
makes this commonality explicit: any algebraic identity that holds in
$\ZZ[g,h]$ — such as the factorisation $\kpoly_w(g,h) = h^e - g^o$ on
the variety $g = h^2 - 1$ — is inherited by every basis that specialises
to that variety.

\subsection*{The polynomial form can characterise individual bases}

That said, the polynomial form can establish non-trivial facts that are
\emph{specific to the $(3,2,1)$ encoding}.  A striking example is the
$2a+1$ composition identity: for the Steiner words $\St(\alpha,1)$ and
$\St(1,\beta)$, the identity
\[
  \Phi_{\St(1,\beta)}\!\bigl(\Phi_{\St(\alpha,1)}(a)\bigr)
  \;=\; \Phi_{\St(\alpha+1,\,\beta+2)}(2a+1)
\]
holds for all $\alpha \geq 1$, $\beta \geq 0$, and all $a$ in the domain
of $\Phi_{\St(\alpha,1)}$.
In~\cite{paper73} we prove — working entirely in $\ZZ[g,h]$ via the
structural polynomial — that among all bases $(g,h,q)$ with $g,h \geq 2$
and $g \neq h$, this identity characterises $(g,h,q) = (3,2,1)$ uniquely.
The proof forces $h = 2$ from the coefficient of $a$, then $g = 3$ by
requiring the constant-term equation to hold for all $\alpha$, and finally
$q = 1$ by substitution.  This is a purely polynomial argument: no
numerical verification, no modular arithmetic, no appeal to orbit dynamics.
The structural polynomial framework is the natural setting for such
uniqueness results.

The abstract framework has limits, however.  It does not, by itself, answer
the question of whether the standard Collatz map $(3,2,1)$ has a non-trivial
cycle.  That question requires understanding the modular arithmetic of
$3x+1$, $x/2$ — the residue structure, the admissibility constraints, the
interplay between the parity of $3^o a + \kpoly_w(3,2)$ and the required
E-steps.

The limits are not total, however.  The structural polynomial does carry
modular information, and only a finite truncation is needed to extract it.
Every monomial $g^{o-1-j} h^{\kappa_j}$ with $j \geq 3$ has $\kappa_j \geq 3$
(since the $\kappa$-tuple is non-decreasing and $\kappa_j \geq j$)
and therefore contributes a multiple of $h^3$ to $\kpoly_w(g,h)$.  The
first three terms alone,
\[
  g^{o-1} + g^{o-2} h^{\kappa_1} + g^{o-3} h^{\kappa_2} \pmod{h^3},
\]
determine $\kpoly_w(g,h) \pmod{h^3}$ completely — for any basis.  At
$(g,h) = (3,2)$ this reads off the mod-$8$ character of the cycle element;
at $(g,h) = (8,3)$ the same truncation reads off the mod-$27$ character.
The polynomial carries both facts simultaneously, as specialisations of a
single identity in $\ZZ[g,h]$.

What the polynomial cannot tell us is whether the mod-$8$ structure of the
$(3,2,1)$ map, or the mod-$27$ structure of the $(8,3,1)$ map, is the
right tool for attacking the cycle question in each case.  Those residue
structures are quite different — the arithmetic accidents that make one
basis tractable need not have any analogue in the other — and the details
almost certainly matter.  The abstract framework reveals the \emph{form}
of the modular constraint; deciding its \emph{utility} requires engaging
with the specific arithmetic of the basis in question.

In short: the abstract path framework is the right language for
understanding \emph{what} cycles are and \emph{how} they relate to one
another across bases.  It is not sufficient on its own for understanding
\emph{whether} a particular cycle exists for a \emph{specified} $q$: for
any parity sequence $w$ and any $(g,h)$, a cycle always exists if one is
prepared to let $q$ be determined by $(w,g,h)$ via the reduced cycle
element identity, but whether that $q$ equals a pre-specified value —
such as $q = 1$ for the standard Collatz map — is a question the abstract
form alone cannot settle.  For that, one must engage with the concrete
arithmetic of the map.  The abstract framework reaches further into that
arithmetic than it might first appear, but both levels of description are
necessary, and they overlap more than the literature typically acknowledges.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The structural polynomial $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ is closely related to the
$k$-parameter appearing in the standard affine representation of Collatz
orbits, where it appears as an entry in the affine transformation matrix.
The Steiner word framework was developed
in~\cite{paper67} as a natural decomposition of Syracuse orbits; the present
paper provides its polynomial algebraic foundation.

The $\sigma$-polynomial $\sigma_w(u,v)$ and its successor operation at complex
roots of unity were introduced in~\cite{paper14}; the present paper situates
them within the broader five-representation framework and makes their
connection to $\kpoly_w(g,h)$ via the change of variables $g = u/v$, $h = v$
explicit.

The composition rule (Theorem~\ref{thm:composition}) and cycle element identity
(Theorem~\ref{thm:cycle}) are applied in the companion paper~\cite{paper73} to
prove a uniqueness theorem for the $2a+1$ Steiner composition identity and to
characterise its domain constraint in terms of $2$-adic valuations.
Structural constraints on Collatz cycles derived from Steiner sentence analysis
appear in~\cite{paper68}.
